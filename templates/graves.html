Graves

Graves’disease is an autoimmune condition affecting the thyroid gland. It causes the thyroid gland to make too much thyroid hormone leading to hyperthyroidism. Anyone can get Graves’ disease, but it is more common in women and in people over 30.

What are the symptoms of Graves’ disease?

Patients with Graves’ disease have extra thyroid hormone in their blood. Common symptoms caused by hyperthyroidism include

  • heat intolerance
  • Increased sweating
  • Tremors
  • Weight loss
  • Anxiety, nervousness
  • Menstrual disturbances
  • Hair loss
  • Fast heart beat
  • Bulging eyes- a condition called Graves’ ophthalmopathy

Graves’ ophthalmopathy or thyroid eye disease is an important feature of Graves disease. It is seen in about one third of people with Graves disease. The autoimmune disease affects muscles and tissues around the eyes and causes bulging of the eyes, watering, redness and pain in the eyes. Smoking increases the risk of eye disease.

What are the causes and risk factors for Graves disease?

Graves disease is more common in women, especially between the ages of 30 and 60 years.Some people may have a family history of Graves disease. Smoking increases the risk of Graves disease

How is Graves’ disease treated?

Treatment for Graves disease involves anti thyroid medication to decrease the production of thyroid hormones in the thyroid gland. Some patients may go into remission and achieve normal thyroid function after using these medications for 1 to 2 years. However many patients may not achieve normal thyroid function with these medications alone. Adverse effects of these medications include rare instances of liver dysfunction, allergy and decreased white blood cell counts.

Radioactive iodine is frequently used in these patients. A small amount of radioactive iodine is given orally which is concentrated by the thyroid gland. This iodine gradually causes shrinkage of the thyroid gland and decreases thyroid hormone levels. However radioactive iodine can worsen eye disease in those with Graves ophthalmopathy. Surgery may be need in a subset of patients, including those with large goiters, patients with severe eye involvement, and patients who cannot be given radioactive iodine.
